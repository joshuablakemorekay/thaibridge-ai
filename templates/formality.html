🎯 When to Use Each Level

☸ Monastic (M)

Use: Only when speaking as a monk, to a monk, or about monastic life

Examples: ฉัน (monk eating), จีวร (robes), บิณฑบาต (alms round)

⚠️ Warning: Never use casually - it's disrespectful to the Sangha

👑 Royal (R)

Use: Only for Thai royalty and in formal royal contexts

Examples: เสวย (eat-royal), ตรัส (speak-royal), เสด็จ (go-royal)

⚠️ Warning: Even when speaking ABOUT the King/Queen, use royal vocabulary

⌛ Obsolete/Archaic (O)

Use: Historical interest only, reading old texts

Examples: Old pronouns and archaic verb forms

⚠️ Warning: Don't use in modern conversation - people won't understand you!

📜 Literary/Poetic (L)

Use: Poetry, literature, formal writing, artistic expression

Examples: พระจันทร์ (moon-poetic), ดวงตะวัน (sun-literary)

⚠️ Warning: Sounds pretentious in casual speech

🔬 Technical/Specialized (T)

Use: Academic, scientific, medical, legal contexts

Examples: อนุภาค (particle-physics), เซลล์ (cell-biology)

✓ Tip: Use with professionals in their fields

🎩 Formal/Deferential (F)

Use: Business meetings, official events, speaking to elders/superiors

Examples: ดิฉัน (I-female formal), ท่าน (you-honorific)

✓ Tip: Safe choice when unsure - shows respect

⚪ Neutral (N)

Use: Everyday polite speech with ครับ/ค่ะ particles

Examples: ผม/ดิฉัน (I), คุณ (you), กิน (eat)

✓ Tip: Use with strangers, service staff, acquaintances - the default

💬 Casual/Colloquial (C)

Use: Friends, family, peers in informal settings

Examples: เออ (yeah), อ่ะ (ok), เหรอ (really?)

⚠️ Warning: Wait for Thai people to shift to this level first

⚠️ Impolite/Rude (I)

Use: ONLY with very close friends who expect it

Examples: กู (I-rude), มึง (you-rude), ไอ้ (prefix-rude)

🚨 DANGER: Can seriously offend if used with wrong people. Be EXTREMELY careful!

💡 Practical Tips for Foreigners

1. Default to Neutral (N)

When in doubt, use neutral formality with ครับ/ค่ะ particles. It's polite and appropriate for 90% of situations.

2. Let Thais Lead on Casual Speech

Don't shift to casual (C) until Thai friends do first. They'll signal when it's appropriate.

3. Respect the Sacred

Never use monastic (M) or royal (R) vocabulary casually. These are deeply respected in Thai culture.

4. Avoid Impolite & Vulgar

Even if you hear Thais using impolite (I) or vulgar (V) words, don't copy them as a foreigner. The rules are different.

5. Context Matters

The same word can be fine in one context and offensive in another. Pay attention to setting and relationships.

6. When at Temples

Use formal (F) vocabulary when speaking to monks or about Buddhist concepts. Show proper respect.

7. Professional Settings

Stick to formal (F) or neutral (N) in business. Technical (T) is appropriate when discussing specialized topics.

8. Literary Words

Literary (L) words are beautiful but sound unnatural in conversation. Save them for writing.
