Unified "Tones & Consonant Classes" section

Thai tones are not written directly — you calculate the tone from three things:
the consonant class, whether the syllable is live or dead, and the tone mark.
The consonant class is the primary factor that determines the tone.

The three consonant classes:
- MID class (9 letters) — the most regular; the only class that makes all 5 tones.
- HIGH class (11 letters) — no mark gives a rising tone; only 3 possible tones.
- LOW class (24 letters) — the tone marks act differently here (the common error).

Live vs dead syllables: a live syllable ends in a long vowel or a sonorant; a
dead syllable ends in a short vowel or a hard p/t/k stop. Live and dead
syllables follow different tone rules.

Special rule — the leading ห: a silent leading ห in front of a single low-class
consonant makes it follow high-class rules, so มา (come, mid tone) becomes
หมา (dog, rising tone).

Practice drills (progressive, levelled): (1) class identification, (2) tone
calculation with mid → high → low → mixed levels, (3) contrast pairs, and
(4) a leading-ห drill. Each correct answer awards XP through the existing
/api/check_answer scoring, so points stay consistent with the other quizzes.
